Ministry of Sound

Elephant & Castle. Sound first — the Ministry, Fridays.

The exhibit

Ministry of Sound had already made itself into an institution before FunkyElite ever put a name on its door. Opened on 21 September 1991 in a disused bus garage at Elephant & Castle, the club was built on Justin Berkmann’s manifesto — "sound system first, lights second, design third" — and inspired by New York’s Paradise Garage. No alcohol at first, no VIP fuss, and a booth that hosted Larry Levan, David Morales, Roger Sanchez and Tony Humphries on the opening runs.

By the 2000s the Fridays had their own identity: ‘Smoove’ — the in-house Friday night that covered the full London spectrum: house, UK garage, R&B, hip hop and dancehall, played in The Box on what remained the most obsessively-engineered sound rig in the city.

On the FunkyElite guest list, the booth ran DJ Steve Sadler alongside DJ Scott Simons and Matt ‘Jam’ Lamont — the UK garage pioneer who, on Kiss FM as Tuff Jam with Karl ‘Tuff Enuff’ Brown, had already helped invent the sound the Ministry’s Fridays celebrated. Guest DJs filled the rest of the bill.

On the record: Ministry of Sound, and how its overspill helped invent UK garage

Ministry of Sound was founded on 21 September 1991 by James Palumbo, Justin Berkmann and Humphrey Waterhouse in a disused bus garage at 103 Gaunt Street, Elephant & Castle. Berkmann had modelled it on New York’s Paradise Garage — a room where the sound system was the point. The club opened without an alcohol licence, past midnight, and with a door policy that assumed you could name half a dozen Chicago house DJs. It worked immediately, and its record label — launched in 1993 — became the second-biggest independent in the world before being sold to Sony in 2016.

There is a direct thread from this room to the sound of its later Fridays. In the early 90s, ravers coming off the Ministry floor would spill into the nearby Elephant & Castle pub, where a young Matt ‘Jam’ Lamont was DJing his Sunday morning ‘Happy Days’ residency. To keep the crowd going, he began playing sped-up dubs of UK house records — which Mixmag credits as the blueprint for UK garage. By the time Matt Jam was playing the Ministry’s Friday ‘Smoove’ nights a decade later, he was doing so in the room whose overspill had helped invent the genre in the first place.
